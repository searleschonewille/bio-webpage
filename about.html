Background

William (Bill) P. Harper was born in Niagara Falls, N. Y. on March 13, 1921 to Canadian parents, a soldier of the First World War and his wife, a nurse. Harper grew up and spent his life in Niagara Falls, Canada. One of Harper's most vivid childhood memories was of building a scale model of a Zeppelin airship out of balsam and paper which had to be burned beside the family house during a bout of Scarlett Fever.

As a teenager, Harper developed an aptitude for mathematics and a talent for baseball. A tall southpaw with a wild fastball, Harper came to be known around the league as the 'Elongated Lefty.' Others called him 'Tex' due to his peculiar and unnaccountable southern drawl. He played for Niagara Falls on a number of championship teams in the Ontario Baseball Association, including the 1936 West End Bantams with lifelong friends Fran Matthews and Bob Cottinger and the 1940 Burroughs Juniors. After the war Harper pitched for the Houcks Senior Baseball Team, which won the Ontario championship in 1946, and received interest from a major league scout who ultimately determined that he was too old to play professional baseball.

Second World War

At the outbreak of the Second World War, Harper enlisted in the Royal Canadian Air Force and entered the pilot officer training school at CFB Trenton. A skilled pilot and marksman, Harper temporarily served as a trainer and drone pilot, until his commanding officer decided that the sound of his drawl over the radio was simply too langorous and stupifying to motivate good shooting. Harper was gladyly transferred to active combat and joined 421 'Red Indian' Squadron of 127 Wing (Fighter Command). Before his departure for the European threatre, Harper met Dorothy Walker of Napanee, Ontario, at a military dance. William Harper and Dorothy Walker married in 1942.
